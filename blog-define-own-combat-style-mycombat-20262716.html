At its core, this process involves auditing your current skill set, identifying gaps in your defense or offense, and curating a syllabus that addresses your real-world scenarios. Many practitioners make the mistake of thinking "freestyle" means "unstructured," but the most effective self-created styles are heavily documented and rigorously tested. The concept relies on three pillars: Selection, Integration, and Iteration. Selection is the conscious choice of techniques based on efficacy rather than tradition. Integration is the seamless blending of these techniques into fluid combinations that make sense biomechanically for your body. Iteration is the continuous process of testing these combinations under pressure, using tools like randomized timers and voice-guided intervals to simulate the chaos of a real encounter. Step by Step

  1. Step 1: Conduct a comprehensive self-audit to identify your primary attributes, such as reach, speed, grappling preference, or striking power, and list the top 5 techniques you feel most confident executing.
  2. Step 2: Map out your "gaps" by analyzing scenarios where you feel vulnerable, then select complementary techniques from other disciplines to fill those holes, ensuring a balanced offensive and defensive toolkit.
  3. Step 3: Build your first "Signature Combination" in MyCombat by programming a specific sequence of 3-5 moves, setting the voice prompts to guide you through the flow, and establishing a baseline timer for repetition.
  4. Step 4: Enter the testing phase by running your custom workout under fatigue conditions, using the random interval generator to disrupt your rhythm, and refining the combination based on where you stumble or lose efficiency.

Common Mistakes

  • Mistake 1: Trying to learn too much too soon. When you define own combat style mycombat strategies emphasize depth over breadth; attempting to master ten different styles at once results in mastering none, leaving you with a "jack of all trades, master of none" dilemma that fails under pressure.
  • Mistake 2: Ignoring the importance of conditioning specific to your new style. Creating a style that relies heavily on high kicks is useless if you haven't trained your hip flexibility and balance specifically for those movements; your training regimen must evolve alongside your technique selection.
